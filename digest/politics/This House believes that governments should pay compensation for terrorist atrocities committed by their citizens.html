This House believes that governments should pay compensation for terrorist atrocities committed by their citizens

Terrorism, according to Wikipedia, is, broadly speaking, “those violent acts which are intended to create fear (terror), are perpetrated for a religious, political or ideological goal, and deliberately target or disregard the safety of civilians or non-combatants”. There is some debate as to whether or not we can distinguish between terrorism and freedom fighters but for the purposes of this debate when we refer to terrorists we refer to groups such as al-Qaeda, Al-Shabaab, Hamas, Hezbollah, the Irish Republican Army, Taliban and other such organisations that are generally recognised as terrorist. Compensation refers to a monetary payment given to the individual who are wither direct victims of a terrorist attacks or else to family members who suffer as a result of the death of a loved one. It would be wise as a proposition to begin the debate by stipulating that this would only be possible where it is clear who the perpetrators were and what their nationality was.

There are two possible scenarios. Either the terrorists have committed the atrocities in their own nation in which case there is less debate as we are far more inclined to believe that governments have certain duties towards their own citizens, or terrorists commit atrocities in countries other than their own, in which case there is a greater burden on the proposition to show why the state ought to be held responsible.

So the questions to be answered in this debate are: Are we justified in holding a state responsible for terrorist acts committed by its citizens? What will the consequences of this policy be?

The UN suggests: The more viable alternative [to the motion under debate] is to create an international fund for victims of terrorism. Terrorism is a global phenomenon and we need a global solution to this menace so that the damage is mitigated to a certain extent. This view of setting up an international fund was even backed by the UN since the government can’t pay every time. This fund was originally suggested to be providing financial support rather than compensation but could be easily adapted will serve better is a more viable alternative instead of calling on the government to pay up for acts of terror.[1]

This is one possible line that an opposition can take. It is also possible for the opposition to argue that the government of the victims should pay the compensation or that there should be no compensation at all. I will run the opposition line that the victim government should be paying compensation.

[1] United Nations, ‘Supporting Victims of Terrorism’, January 2009, http://www.un.org/en/terrorism/ctitf/pdfs/un_report_supporting_victims_terrorism.pdf

 

Caitlin Macleod
Responsibility
For
Point: 

The state must pay compensation for the terrorist attacks because they are indirectly responsible for them. It is the state’s duty to ensure that its citizens act within the law by providing deterrents, education and by preventing crimes through the use of intelligence. Even if the terrorist atrocities do not take place within the state, they were most probably planned within the state or initiated by someone who lives in the state. This means that it was within the state’s power to have prevented the attack from taking place. Just as we would expect a state to investigate and track down a drug syndicate, we expect the state to track down terrorist organisations and prevent their activities.

In many cases, states are not only responsible in this manner but also in a more direct manner.

This may involve a paramilitary group – a group that acts like a military but whose affiliation with the state or whose existence is kept secret. It may involve the state supplying the terrorist group with weaponry, money or intelligence. In these cases the state should clearly be held liable for the atrocities committed. For example, Iran supports Hamas financially and also claims that it shares identical views on Palestine with Hamas.[1] Iranian President Mahmoud Ahmadinejad vowed to keep supporting Hamas until the collapse of Israel.[2] Syrian President Bashar Assad as another example provides both funds and weaponry for Hezbollah.[3]

Making the state pay for the wrongs of its citizens is therefore not only just but sends the message that the international community recognises the states involvement and duty in this regard. This means that the state becomes responsible in the sense that it takes the blame.

[2] “Ahmadinejad: Iran will support Hamas until collapse of Israel.” Haaretz. 2011. http://www.haaretz.com/news/ahmadinejad-iran-will-support-hamas-until-collapse-of-israel-1.253714

[3] Karam, Zeina. “Hezbollah Supports Syria Leader Bashar Assad.” Huffington Post. 2011. http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2011/05/25/hezbollah-syria-bashar-assad_n_867032.html

 

 

Counterpoint: 

It is unjust to expect the state to prevent terrorist attacks. Most of the terror attacks are covert operations which are totally outside the purview of the government in the sense that the ruling mechanism has no idea that attacks are being planned. Why should any government be responsible for acts which it hasn't endorsed, authorised or has no knowledge of whatsoever? Terrorist organisations are complex and difficult to track down. They spread themselves in a variety of locations. The structure of most terrorist organisations is such that individuals only communicate with their immediate superior and do not know the identity or whereabouts of any other member of the organisation, making it very difficult to gather intelligence. Another reason why it is difficult to track down terrorists is their unwillingness to offer up information. Terrorists are in many cases willing to die for their cause and so there is very little that we can do to persuade them to give up other members of the organisation or their plans. In short, they work very hard at hiding their plans and their members. It is a testament to this fact the US government, with their sophisticated means of gathering intelligence, took years to locate Osama Bin Laden.  

Secondly, it is only rarely that we are certain of who committed a terrorist attack and what their nationality was.  How difficult would it be, then, to determine whether or not the state was assisting such an organisation? This is very difficult to prove. The Lockerbie bombing took place in 1988 and it was only in 2011 that the Libyan president confessed that he had ordered the attacks. Even with a massive investigation it was not possible to prove his involvement until this confession. 

Lastly, and very importantly, states are only responsible for the security of their own citizens and only have a duty to punish criminal acts within their own borders. The state is therefore not responsible and should not be required to pay compensation.

We would hold instead that it is the individuals who commit, plan and command the atrocities that should be held exclusively responsible. 

Incentivising state action
For
Point: 

If states are expected to pay compensation in this way then they are incentivised to take action against terrorist organisations to prevent incurring this massive cost. They are more likely to put resources into investigating leads and to take a hard stance on terrorist activity. This will decrease the prevalence of terrorism or at least make more intelligence available.

Why would the expectations that a country should pay compensation be a strong enough motive to force nations to pay up? If it becomes commonplace and therefore expected, nations who do not pay compensation will be viewed as supporting terrorism or perhaps be suspected of being in cahoots with the terrorists. This would be very harmful to the nation. For example, in the case of the Lockerbie bombing, suspicion of Libya’s involvement lead to sanctions and poor international relations for many years. Libya ultimately felt it was better to pay $2.7billion, $10million per person killed, than continue to face UN sanctions.[1]

[1] BBC News, ‘Q&A: The Lockerbie compensation deal’, 14 August 2003, http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/uk/3150793.stm

 

Counterpoint: 

Once again, this is unlikely to decrease terrorism because, as we explained, it is so very difficult to track down terrorists. Even if the state wants to deter terrorism they may not be able to.

Secondly, the fear of having to pay this compensation will make states more likely to try to hide terrorist activities in their nations and shelter terrorists who they know to have committed atrocities. They may deny any knowledge of the terrorist activities or even frame or blame other countries or terrorist organisations.  This is certainly harmful to the war on terror. 

Prevents revenge violence
For
Point: 

Compensation can prevent more violence because the victims are less likely to seek revenge if they feel that a) they have been recognised as victims b) the perpetrators (at least someone representing the perpetrators) has suffered financially and so some justice has been done c) they are more satisfied with the outcome of events because they have received money. This is less important in scenarios such as the London train bombings and 9/11 where there is little chance of victims retaliating but it is important in conflicts that have revenge as a common theme. For example, the Israel-Palestine conflict involves a lot of retaliation resembling age-old family feuds. Feuds are often prevented through the payment of blood money, in Saudi Arabia blood money rates are 100,000 Riyals to compensate for the death of a Muslim man.[1] Perhaps we could use similar compensation to prevent some of this retaliation by compensating victims in the case of terrorism victims. 

[1] Hürriyet, ‘Paying blood money to prevent blood feuds’, Hürriyet Daily News, 7 July 2009, http://www.hurriyetdailynews.com/n.php?n=paying-blood-money-to-prevent-b...

 

Counterpoint: 

The Arab-Hebrew conflict, which started as a political and nationalist conflict over competing territorial ambitions cannot just be solved by paying some money and sweeping everything under the carpet. If this were to be the case, this conflict would have been long over and every crime against humanity also for that matter could be brushed aside by paying compensation. Both sides have a particular aim that they are fighting for which does not disappear with compensation.

Compensation cannot ease the sufferings and also may not bring down the desire for revenge in any case. Post-9/11 there was a leap of 1600% in hate crimes towards Muslims throughout the United States.[1] The anti-Muslim sentiment that boils not far below the surface in US society has not disappeared since Osama Bin Laden was killed.[2] In short, justice and punishment of perpetrators does not remove the sense of suffering and anger when someone has experienced a tragic loss. 

[1] Schevitz, Tanya, ‘FBI sees leap in anti-Muslim hate crimes / 9/11 attacks blamed for bias -- blacks still most frequent victims’, SFGate.com, 26 November 2002, http://articles.sfgate.com/2002-11-26/news/17570762_1_crime-incidents-crime-victims-african-americans

[2] Garofoli, Joe, ‘Anti-Muslim sentiment grows 9 years later’, SFGate.com, 22 September 2010, http://articles.sfgate.com/2010-09-11/news/23998489_1_muslims-and-islam-anti-muslim-sentiment-islamic

 

Cultural reconciliation
For
Point: 

Often when a terrorist attack occurs the victims feel anger not only towards the individual perpetrators or the terrorist organisation but towards the entire national, political, cultural or religious group they belong to. An example of this is the flood of anti-Muslim, anti-Arab sentiment that arose after the 9/11 attacks.[1] This has a lot of negative consequences because it means that perfectly innocent people will suffer hate crimes and ostracization which in turns is likely to turn those people against the victims of the terrorist attack. This kind of disharmony lays the foundation for poor international relations, discrimination and particularly make it difficult for integration to occur such a when a Muslim family moves to the US.

When the state pays compensation for the terrorist attacks they are making a statement that they recognise terrorism to be wrong. When this happens victims begin to distinguish from the terrorist organisation and the rest of the people in their cultural or national group. This allows for greater cultural reconciliation.

[1] “Islamaphobia.” Wikipedia. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamophobia

 

Counterpoint: 

This is false. When the state pays compensation they are taking responsibility for the crime, as the proposition stated in their first argument, and in doing so they are associating themselves with the terrorists, not disassociating themselves as prop claims. Furthermore, if the state wanted to condemn terrorism they could simply release a statement doing so. This is a frank contradiction with props first argument. 

Precedent for reparations
For
Point: 

The times when reparations tend to be suggested are when there is a wound of sorts between two groups of people that needs to be healed in order for there to be closure and reconciliation. An example of this is after the Truth and Reconciliation Commission in South Africa when compensation was given to victims of apartheid.[1] We would argue that terrorist attacks fit perfectly into this frame because terrorist attacks tend to be committed by one group with a particular political, religious or national identity against another group with a different political, religious or national identity. Compensation plays a role in reconciling these two groups.

This compensation is not purely to provide the victims with things they have lost e.g. if they have lost the breadwinner of their family money will help them and if their home was burnt down again money will help. Compensation from one’s own government can fulfil this need. But compensation from another government acts not only to fill financial needs but to provide reconciliation and closure.

[1] “Truth and Reconciliation Commission (South Africa).” Wikipedia. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_and_Reconciliation_Commission_(South_Africa)

 

Counterpoint: 

An injection of money does not reconcile a family to the death of a loved one. In South Africa there are still tensions between white and black people and some continue to call for a white tax and land reclaim so there is certainly not closure.

Furthermore, this does not seem to be especially important when the conflict is between two nations that are far apart. It is unclear why citizens of America need to feel friendly towards Middle Easterners if this doesn’t affect international relations. 

Practicality
Against
Point: 

It is often very difficult to identify the perpetrator of crimes. This is partly because the perpetrators are often at great pains to conceal their identity and partly because the perpetrators often die in the terrorist attack e.g. suicide bombers. For example, in the case of the Lockerbie bombing both the Guardians of the Islamic Revolution and the Islamic Jihad Organisation claimed responsibility via telephone. It took a further three years to find someone guilty.[1] The identification of the perpetrators of 9/11 required ‘the largest criminal enquiry in the history of the United States’.[2] They had to follow half a million leads. There will be many, many cases where it is impossible to identify the perpetrators, particularly where nations do not have the resources that the US does to carry out these investigations.

[1] “Pan Am Flight 103.” Wikipedia. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pan_Am_Flight_103#Claims_of_responsibility

[2] “Hijackers in the September 11 Attacks.” Wikipedia. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hijackers_in_the_September_11_attacks

 

Counterpoint: 

The perpetrators of both the Lockerbie bombing and 9/11 were identified and tried. It seems that it is perfectly possible to identify perpetrators and we do not feel that it is problematic that these things take time and resources because it has a positive outcome. In the case of the Lockerbie bombing, each family received US$10 million in compensation. [1]

[1] “Pan Am Flight 103.” Wikipedia. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pan_Am_Flight_103#Claims_of_responsibility

 

State compensation detracts from individual responsibility
Against
Point: 

Once a state has paid compensation the international community will consider the matter settled and justice done and the state will carry the shame of the act. This detracts from the fact that we need to hold the individual perpetrators responsible and that it is still very important to bring them to justice and also to track down other members of the organisation who are still active. 

Counterpoint: 

This is not the case. The international community is not so ignorant and forgetful as you would have it and the victims of these atrocities and their families and friends would certainly be active in calling for the capture and punishment of the perpetrators and the rest of the terrorist organisation even if compensation had been paid. Surely, assuming but not conceding that we buy your argument, having the victim state pay compensation to its own citizens as opp suggests will too detract from individual responsibility. 

Harms to other citizens
Against
Point: 

The state which pays the compensation would be using money provided by taxpayers who have done nothing wrong and are not terrorists and who would otherwise benefit from this tax through public services. This is unfair when it is not they who have committed the atrocity. Furthermore, by expecting compensation from the state, when a state is not able or willing to pay compensation, its citizens suffer from the countries poor reputation when there are sanctions and other ramifications. It would be better for us to shift the paradigm to focus on individual responsibility of the perpetrators to avoid further injustice towards innocent civilians. 

Counterpoint: 

This is a lot fairer than having tax payers of the victim nation paying compensation as they are even further removed from the cause.

Furthermore, we believe it is acceptable for states to use taxpayers’ money to set up prisons and courts to punish criminals even though citizens are not responsible for the acts of those criminals and so it is not the case that we never allow states to spend taxpayers’ money on problems that taxpayers’ never caused. This is just the unfortunate reality of tax.

There is no precedent for this
Against
Point: 

When the citizens of a state commit a crime that victimises people in other countries we do not expect their government to pay compensation to the victim even if the crime is something as large a human rights violation as rape or murder. Even when criminals commit crimes against people in their own country states are not required to pay compensation – even though the victims are their own citizens who they have a duty to.

In some wars such as the two world wars and the gulf war, reparations are demanded but it is not by any means the case in every war.

There are in fact very few cases where we expect governments to pay compensations for their citizens or their own actions. It seems bizarre then that we should require it in this circumstance.

Counterpoint: 

Precedent does not prove something right or wrong. All it proves is that it there are similar things that a lot of people agree with. The burden on the opposition is not to decide whether or not the motion fits in with other things most people think should be done but to show that this specific motion falls. Furthermore, it is not the propositions burden to present an opinion on war reparations and compensation for other crimes.

Furthermore, assuming but not conceding that there is no precedent for compensation, it makes no sense that the victim nation pays compensation either. In short, opposition is contradicting itself. 

Benefits of international fund
Against
Point: 

An international fund will mark this as a global problem and encourage a global anti-terrorist culture whereby, instead of blaming particular governments and thereby creating animosity, the international community works together at finding solutions.[1] The development of this co-operative culture is important not only for the challenging of terrorism but also for solving other global problems such as climate change and human trafficking which simply cannot be dealt with by any one government but only by the world as a whole. Furthermore, those who receive compensation from such a fund will feel the support of the global community and this will foster feelings of safety that are so challenged by terrorism.

[1] Ki-Moon, Ban, “Opening Symposium on Victims of Terrorism”, United Nations, 9 September 2008, http://www.un.org/News/Press/docs/2008/sg2142.doc.htm

 

Counterpoint: 

You wildly overestimate the effects of this international fund. Firstly, unless you make it obligatory for people to donate (which will be impossible to enforce) many nations will simply choose not to and therefore there will not be the global participation you want. Even if everyone did donate, it is difficult to see how this would really encourage co-operation in other areas. There is no real interaction required but merely a financial transfer. Furthermore, it seems that it is important that we foster animosity against nations that support terrorists. This is an important statement for the international community to make – that we will not tolerate terrorism  - and we should make these nations lives difficult.
