This House believes that Israel should lift the blockade of Gaza

Israel has maintained a land, air, and sea blockade on the Gaza Strip since June of 2007 when Hamas took control of the Palestinian region. Egypt also maintained a blockade on Gaza from its border until 2011 when it decided to allow people but not aid to go through. The Israeli government maintains that the blockade is necessary for security reasons: Hamas' charter expresses a desire to eradicate the state of Israel, Hamas has launched rockets from Gaza at Israeli civilians, and Hamas uses the ports to smuggle in weapons which are believed to be for use against Israel. In 2010, a Turkish flotilla attempted to break the naval blockade of Gaza claiming that it violated international law1. Facing this and other international pressure, Israel significantly eased the blockade in 2010, allowing all non-military items in upon inspection. Opponents have continued to criticize the blockade because of the economic and humanitarian harm it poses to the 1.5 million people living in the Gaza Strip, with some calling it a "humanitarian crisis" and others calling it a PR disaster for Israel. The arguments for both sides of the debate are presented below.

Bargaining tool
Against
Point: 

If Israel is going to concede control over the Gaza Strip, it's only reasonable to first expect something comparatively valuable in return. Why let go of one of Israel's most powerful bargaining tools? Let them lift the blockade in exchange for the safe return of the captured Israeli soldier GiladShalit, or for an end to rocket fire at Israeli civilians, or for a formal recognition of the state of Israel. To ease the blockade without demanding anything in return is a tactical error, dropping one of Israel's most valuable bargaining tools without exacting anything in return.

Counterpoint: 

Lifting an illegal siege is the sort of thing that should be done because it should be done, not because it is politically or economically expedient. You can't only uphold international law when it's convenient or in your own interests. On top of this, Israel still stands a lot to gain from lifting the blockade without asking for anything in return. Someone has to make the first concession, and this gesture of good will is sure to improve Israel's credibility in negotiations with the Palestinians.

Humanitarian Concerns
For
Point: 

Israel's blockade of Gaza has caused a serious humanitarian crisis. As a result of Israel's actions, Gaza's economy is stagnant and has continued to plummet since Israel restricted its movement of goods and people.1 As long as Israel maintains its limitations on Gaza's exports and commercial production, the people of the Gaza strip will remain dependent on humanitarian aid, suffering from a 50% unemployment rate where limitations on entry and exit of the area means that chronically ill people are sometimes unable to seek outside treatment and families are torn apart.2 Education levels are low, and the whole region is in a sense imprisoned. The UN Human Rights Council acknowledged this in 2008 and called for Israel to lift the siege for humanitarian reasons.3

3 http://www.unhchr.ch/huricane/huricane.nsf/view01/7A7B2B76C0F3C3F6C12573... [4]

Counterpoint: 

This is simply no longer the case. A senior Red Cross official said this week that Gaza is not suffering from a humanitarian crisis, and that the main problems instead relate to infrastructure and access to certain goods1. Israel has already dramatically eased its blockade of Gaza (since 2010) and now almost all consumer goods are allowed in.There is no shortage of food, construction projects have resumed, dozens of building and infrastructure projects have begun2.

On top of this, it's not clear that lifting the blockade will do much for the actual people living in the Hamas-controlled Gaza strip. Most of the money they earn could easily be redirected to weapons while the people remain as poor as ever. It's not like it was a rich area before the blockade. Not to mention that people can now pass through the Egyptian border.

International Pressure
For
Point: 

Mounting international consensus suggests that the blockade is cruel and illegitimate. Even the United States, Israel's staunchest ally, criticized the blockade as untenable and has urged Israel to find an alternative means of ensuring its security.1

As long as newspapers can continue to publish images of starving Gazans and "Freedom Flotillas," Israel's reputation in the international arena will suffer tremendously. Israel could benefit from continued economic, diplomatic, and military support from other countries, so it cannot afford to jeopardize its relations with other countries by maintaining the blockade. Lifting the blockade could even improve Israel's security. In the wake of the revolution in Egypt the new government has brought back police throught the country except in northen Sinai. With no police there has been a surge of smuggling into Gaza with up to 250 cars a week going through tunnels.2 Lifting the blockade could persuade Egypt to bring back the police and reduce the amount of smuggling while at the same time allowing more aid and legal goods in.

1 http://www.nytimes.com/2010/06/03/world/middleeast/03policy.html?hp [7]
2 http://www.nytimes.com/2011/08/15/world/middleeast/15sinai.html [8]

Counterpoint: 

Sometimes what is right is not what is popular.No matter what it does, many people and countries have hated and will continue to hate Israel regardless of whether or not it lifts the blockade because of a complex matrix of economic, religious, cultural and political factors. Lifting the blockade will not change this.

International Law
For
Point: 

Israel's blockade of Gaza has resulted in the starvation of civilians in Gaza and can be seen as collective punishment, both of which are illegal under international law.1 According to Richard Falk, the situation is still a form of collective punishment despite the 2010 easing of the blockade2. By restricting construction materials from entering Gaza, their economic recovery has stagnated and factory and construction workers remain unemployed2.

Clearly, easing the restrictions has been insufficient. Israel must comply with international humanitarian law and lift the blockade completely.

1 http://www.reuters.com/article/2010/06/05/us-israel-flotilla-un-idUSTRE6... [9]
2 http://www.ohchr.org/en/NewsEvents/Pages/DisplayNews.aspx?NewsID=10871&L... [10]

Counterpoint: 

Israel has every right to protect itself under international law, and this includes the use of a blockade.1 The circumstances are akin to when the United States and Britain blockaded Japan and Germany in World War II, which was considered legal.1 Under international law, a maritime blockade is a recognized and legitimate tool that can be used during an armed conflict, which is the case between Israel and Gaza2.

The law of blockade was codified in the 1909 Declaration of London and updated in the 1994 San Remo Manual on International Law Applicable to Armed Conflicts at Sea.3 Because Hamas rules the Gaza Strip and Israel and Hamas have been in an intractable conflict for years3, the blockade is legal and thereby a legitimate self-defence tactic.

As you will see later, Hamas itself is on the wrong side of international law. There really is no clear moral high ground in this situation and it's patently unfair to hold only one party accountable, especially when all signs suggest Israel's actions are legal.

Make Peace more likely
For
Point: 

Hamas essentially has veto power over any potential peace deal between Israel and the Palestinians. While Hamas is in power, Israel needs to be able to have a working relationship with it for a deal to be reached and upheld.1 As long as Israel maintains the blockade of Gaza, Palestinians will not trust Israel in a deal, and the prospects for long term peace will remain elusive. Lifting the blockade is a critical and necessary sign of good faith on Israel's behalf.

1 http://www.usnews.com/opinion/blogs/erbe/2010/09/03/gazas-problems-make-... [14]

Counterpoint: 

As long as Hamas is in control of the Gaza strip, there can be no lasting peace. Hamas has demonstrated repeatedly over the years that it is not a trustworthy partner for peace. Hamas has explicitly called for the destruction of Israel. Just this summer, Hamas Prime Minister asked people to pray for the end of the state of Israel.1 The problem is not Israel's blockade of Gaza, it is the Hamas and the hard-held hatred of the people who elected Hamas democratically.

Removing the blockade could easily escalate tensions or lead to war in the region, as it would give Hamas more of a chance to launch an increasing number of rockets at Israel.

Moral Duty
Against
Point: 

Under ideal circumstances, there would be no need for a blockade. But the primary duty of a state is to protect its people, so Israel is doing what it must. Given the fact that Hamas rockets have repeatedly threatened the lives of Israelis, the government of has a responsibility to stop rockets from the Gaza Strip from coming into Israel and killing civilians. The only way to achieve this is through a blockade. The blockade might impose an economic threat to Gaza, but it is morally justified when it protects Israeli civilians from the very real threat of deadly rocket fire. A blockade might cause some economic stagnation, but economic coercion is better than more violent alternatives, and is an important and legitimate element of Israel's security apparatus."1

On top of this, Hamas has also violated international law on several occasions. The Hamas militants, who targeted Israeli civilians (including women and children) on a daily basis by launching Qassam and Grad rockets, violated the provisions of Common Article 3.2 Hamas may also be responsible for the use of human shields, threatening violence to terrify civilians, the use of indiscriminate attacks, use of prohibited weapons, and locating military centres in civilians centres, among other things.2 Hamas may also be violating international law in its treatment of captured Israeli solider GiladShalit, who they've held hostage for five years without allowing a single visit from an international humanitarian agency.3

2 http://www.globallawforum.org/ViewPublication.aspx?ArticleId=99 [17]
3 http://cnpublications.net/2010/06/25/hamas-violates-international-law/ [18]

Counterpoint: 

The blockade on Gaza does nothing to protect the people of Israel and everything to harm the people of the Gaza Strip. While rockets continue to reach Israel, 1.5 million people in Gaza are suffering. This is not just, not proportionate, and causes far too much suffering to be moral.

The fact that Hamas is also guilty of immoral acts does not justify Israel's blockade. It should strive to do what is right, not sink to the purported standard of its opponents.

Security from Hamas
Against
Point: 

The blockade of Gaza is a necessary security measure for the people in Israel. When Israel gave Gaza back its land and forcibly expelled over 8000 Jews from their homes in exchange for peace, they were met instead with thousands of Qassam rockets aimed at civilian.1

Since July alone, 26 rockets were fired from Gaza into southern Israel. This rocket fire2 contradicts international law because it targets civilians and puts innocent lives at risk. Hamas has shown that it cannot be trusted with a peace agreement and will not stop trying to destroy the state of Israel in any way it can. As such, it's only reasonable for Israel to use the economic coercion of a blockade to prevent Hamas from smuggling in more weapons and materials to use against Israel.3

If 26 rockets hit Israel from Gaza in July, it's hard to see how the blockade serves as an effective security policy for Israel. Particularly now that the Egyptian side is open, Gaza will be able to trade and smuggle weapons. Where there's a will there's a way- to reduce rockets, you need to reduce the will for them, and that means allowing the people of Gaza their freedom.-it predates the rockets1

Weaken Hamas
For
Point: 

The blockade on Gaza was originally created largely to weaken Hamas, but in practice it has effectively strengthened it. Since 2007, Hamas has developed a large network of tunnels and the smuggling business along the Egyptian border has grown exponentially.1 This had led to tremendous financial gain for Hamas and undermines the legitimate forces in Gaza's civil society.2In terms of political power, the blockade of Gaza may actually serve to embolden Hamas by convincing the people there that Israel is a bully and cannot be trusted.

2 http://www.haaretz.com/news/un-official-israel-siege-on-gaza-encourages-... [22]

Counterpoint: 

To end the blockade without any concessions from Hamas can only serve to strengthen Hamas, both in terms of economic and political capital. It re-enforces politically that Hamas' immoral, hard-line, terrorist tactics can succeed at achieving tactical concessions from Israel. It also makes it easier for Hamas to finance its activities by facilitating trade upon which they charge taxes. This is dangerous for Israel and for the world- after all, Hamas is the very group that condemned the capture of Osama Bin Laden and praised him as a holy warrior.1
