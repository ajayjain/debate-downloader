This house would restrict the use of ‘fracking’ to extract natural gas from shale

The precautionary principle has no universally accepted definition, the 1992 Rio Declaration on Environment and Development defined it as 'Where there are threats of serious or irreversible environmental damage, lack of full scientific certainty shall not be used as a reason for postponing cost effective measures to prevent environmental degradation.'[1]  Shale gas is a term used to describe natural gas trapped as hydrocarbons within saturated rock formations, especially the sedimentary rock shale. Although quite widespread, until the past decade or so shale gas has not been much exploited as an energy source as it cannot be extracted easily and cheaply by traditional drilling techniques. In recent years this has changed as higher energy prices have made investment in shale gas fields more worthwhile, and improved extraction techniques have meant that unconventional sources of energy can now be exploited more easily. The main process involved in extracting shale gas is hydraulic fracturing (“fracking”), where a fluid is forced down a well to create a build-up of pressure in the rock below, forcing it to break up and allowing the gas contained within it to be recovered to the surface.

Shale gas formations can be found across much of the Eastern and Central parts of North America. Exploitation first took off in 2000; the number of wells in the Barnett shales of Texas grew from less than 600 to over 9000[2] in the eight years to 2008. Other deposits being exploited include the Marcellus shales of New York and adjacent states, and the Haynesville shales in Louisiana and Texas. There is now interest in exploiting similar deposits across the border in Quebec, as well as in Poland and the UK within Europe.

Proponents of shale gas argue that it can provide a vast new source of reliable and economical energy, as well as helping industrial economies move towards a lower-carbon future. But sceptics doubt the economic viability of the sector, question its environmental impact, and raise serious safety concerns. These voices are becoming increasingly influential in the USA, and are attempting to block the exploitation of shale gas in Canada and Europe.

Human health should come before material gain.
For
Point: 

The residents of areas where shale gas is found and drilling may occur have a right to maintain their health, something which drilling may affect. Dangerous chemicals are pumped into rock formations that are also the source of groundwater for millions of people. In 2008 a hydrologist found benzene in a water well in Wyoming above where fracking was occurring.[1] Dramatic film of inflammable gas leaking through drinking water pipes underlines the risks posed by this uncontrolled experiment under our homes.[2] Companies are in denial over these dangers, but their refusal to come clean about exactly what chemicals they are using is hardly reassuring. While there is a chance that lives are at risk, either from water contamination or from gas explosions as a result of the fracking such risky methods of extracting natural gas should be banned.

[1] Gjelten, Tom, ‘Water Contamination Concerns Linger for Shale Gas’, npr, 23 September 2009, http://www.npr.org/templates/story/story.php?storyId=113142234

[2] Lustgarten, Abrahm, ‘Does Natural Gas Drilling Make Water Burn’, Scientific American, 27 April 2009, http://www.scientificamerican.com/article.cfm?id=natural-gas-make-water-...

 

Counterpoint: 

Shale gas extraction is environmentally responsible and safe for human health. 95% or more of the fluid pumped into wells as part of the fracking process is ordinary water, and most of the remaining 5% is naturally-occurring sand, vegetable guar (a gum derived from beans) and nitrogen.[1] These are pumped deep underground in order to stimulate the release of gas, and then removed again at the well-head as part of the extraction process. Any fracking fluids left within the rock are so far underground that there is no danger of any contamination of groundwater sources. In the UK the Environment Agency’s “staff consider that there is a very low risk from these chemicals because there is no groundwater in or around the deep shale formation and there is a low risk of the chemicals migrating upwards and having an adverse impact on the environment.”[2] Indeed, no evidence has ever been presented of the fracking fluids getting into the water supply and if they did they could equally come from other consumer and industrial applications of the fluids,[3] while the gas could have occurred naturally.

The Precautionary Principle should be applied.
For
Point: 

Given that some of the negative consequences from hydraulic fracturing processes may take many years to emerge, the precautionary principle should be applied. Applying the precautionary principle would mean changing the burden of proof from environmentalists having to prove that fracking does cause harm in each circumstance to it being the oil and gas firms who have to prove that fracking is safe.[1] As a result fracking should be stopped all together or at the very least greatly reducing the amount of fracking leaving only a few fields in operation so that the effects of the process can be studied and as a result sufficient legislation but in place to regulate the industry.[2] Until it can be prove these methods are entirely safe, hydraulic fracturing should be stopped and the shale gas industry denied further drilling permits.

[1] Pollan, Michael, ‘The Importance of the Precautionary Principle’, New York Times, 2001, http://environmentalcommons.org/precaution-importance.pdf

[2] Public Health Law Network, ‘Hydraulic Fracturing: Should “Fracking” Be a Dirty Word?’, 20 April 2011, http://www.publichealthlawnetwork.org/hydraulic-fracturing-should-fracki...

 

Counterpoint: 

The precautionary principle simply wants ‘cost effective measures to prevent environmental degradation.’[1] This does not mean that fracking should have to stop in order to apply the principle. Rather as the oil and gas companies are already doing wherever possible preventing environmental degradation. The European Commission says that the precautionary principle should ‘never justify arbitrary decisions.’[2] But that is exactly what it would be doing if it was invoked to ban a whole industry just because we do not know every consequence of fracking.

[1] United Kingdom Interdepartmental Liaison Group on Risk Assessment (UK-ILGRA), ‘The Precautionary Principle: Policy and Application’, Health and Safety Executive, http://www.hse.gov.uk/aboutus/meetings/committees/ilgra/pppa.htm

[2] European Commission, ‘The precautionary principle’, Europa.eu, 2 February 2000, http://europa.eu/legislation_summaries/consumers/consumer_safety/l32042_...

 

Extraction of shale gas is damaging to the environment.
For
Point: 

Expanding shale gas extraction would be very damaging to the environment as the process requires a large number of small wells to be drilled in each gas field, Ohio for example has 80,000 fracked wells[1], by comparison only 10,000 have been drilled in the UK part of the North Sea since 1964.[2] This means beautiful rural areas will be spoiled by the presence of many gas rigs, each with its accompanying pipes, buildings, tracks, etc. Agricultural and amenity land will be lost and wildlife disturbed. And heavy construction traffic will ruin roads and create noise pollution for nearby residents.

[1] Jim, ’80,000 Fracked Wells in Ohio – Zero Groundwater Contamination’, Marcellus Drilling News, 10 February 2011, http://marcellusdrilling.com/2011/02/80000-fracked-wells-in-ohio-zero-gr...

[2] The Telegraph, ‘North Sea oil safety report: the UK oil industry in figures’, 6 January 2011, http://www.telegraph.co.uk/finance/newsbysector/energy/8241962/North-Sea...

 

Counterpoint: 

All forms of energy production place some burden on the environment, but compared to any other form of fossil fuel shale gas is limited in its impact. It does not produce huge open-cast pits like tar sands or level mountains like coal-mining, it does not need massive offshore rigs like oil wells or traditional gas extraction, and it does not pose the risk of oil spills like those in the Gulf of Mexico and the Niger Delta. Indeed, many people also object to renewable energy sources such as massive solar arrays, ranks of noisy wind-turbines against the horizon, or hydro-electric schemes that drown valleys and wreck river systems. With proper regulation, the impact of shale gas wells can be made acceptable to local communities, while better horizontal drilling techniques mean fewer wells will be required in the future than in the past, in Alaska horizontal drilling has meant that wells are only needed every four miles.[1]

[1] Pratt, Sara, ‘A Fresh Angle on Oil Drilling’, Geotimes, March 2004, http://www.agiweb.org/geotimes/mar04/feature_horizdrill.html

 

Shale gas does not help prevent climate change.
For
Point: 

Climate change is the biggest problem facing our planet and it requires urgent action for us to shift to a low-carbon economy. Shale gas is simply another fossil fuel, releasing carbon into the atmosphere when it is burnt to provide power. As the Tyndall Centre warns “While it is possible that shale gas could substitute for coal, within the UK, this would likely be counteracted by global use of coal and shale gas.”[1] So rather than replacing even dirtier coal as a source of power, it is likely simply to augment it, feeding our wasteful society’s insatiable appetite for energy. Indeed, if shale gas lowers the price of fossil fuels, it may contribute further to the problem by encouraging a continued rise in energy consumption.[2] Worse, by taking investment from renewable energy sources such as solar, tidal and wind energy, shale gas may postpone the necessary shift to a low-carbon economy.

[1] Wood, et al., ‘Shale gas: a provisional assessment of climate change and environmental impacts’, The Tyndall Centre, January 2011, http://www.tyndall.ac.uk/sites/default/files/coop_shale_gas_report_final..., pp.50-51

[2] Ibid, p.50

 

Counterpoint: 

Shale gas provides an important route to a low-carbon economy. Burning gas to generate electricity for lighting houses and powering factories (and also perhaps increasingly for powering “plug-in” automobiles) emits much less carbon than burning coal in traditional power-stations. At the same time, shale gas can provide much more energy in the next twenty years than renewable sources such as solar, wind and tidal energy can at present offer. So if we are serious about shutting down major polluters such as coal-burning power stations and petrol-drive automobiles, it is shale gas that can provide us with a realistic and affordable lower-carbon solution. Even if targets for renewable generation of electricity are met there will still need to be electricity generation by other sources and gas is the best option.[1] The environmental case for shale gas can be further strengthened by the use of carbon sequestration  whereby new technology is employed to capture the carbon emissions as gas is burnt and to store it in underground rock formations, so that it is not released into the atmosphere, Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory is even researching how to do this in shale.[2]

[1] 9th Transatlantic Energy Governance Dialogue, ‘Shale Gas: A Game Changer for European Energy Security?’ Central European University, 12-13 May 2011, http://www.globalenergygovernance.net/fileadmin/media/events/2011/event_..., p.8

[2] Staff Writers, ‘Livermore Lab and American Shale Oil team to study carbon sequestration’, Energy Daily, 17 December 2008, http://www.energy-daily.com/reports/Livermore_Lab_And_American_Shale_Oil...

 

The free market dictates that we should use shale gas
Against
Point: 

Despite the current low price of gas the long-term trends are bright for shale gas. The supply of easily exploited conventional sources seems to have peaked, while the rapid growth of the Chinese economy has greatly increased demand. As populous countries such as India, Brazil and Indonesia follow China in development and rising prosperity, continued growth in demand for energy will ensure gas prices rise. Shale gas plays are started paying off during the boom years up to the 2008 crash with high initial yields and stable long-term production rates that can be reliably predicted through decline-curve equations. Meanwhile the costs of exploration and production continue to fall as a result of technological improvements. Even with prices around $4Mcf (thousand cubic feet) some projects such as the Marcellus Shale are profitable and prices are expected to begin rising again.[1] All this means that investments in shale gas will pay off handsomely for both governments and private investors.

[1] Phillips, Susan, ‘Marcellus Gas Still Turns a Profit Despite Price Decline, StateImpact, 8 September 2011, http://stateimpact.npr.org/pennsylvania/2011/09/08/marcellus-gas-most-profitable-say-industry-analysts/

 

Counterpoint: 

The economics of shale gas rest upon a number of dubious premises, making it a risky bet both for private investors and for governments thinking of offering the industry incentives and tax breaks. Drilling for unconventional gas is an expensive business, requiring large sums to be laid out on leases for the land, drilling equipment, hydraulic fracturing processes, pipelines, etc. Some wells generate ultimately generate too little gas to pay for their overheads, so a lot of wells have to be drilled for any investment to pay off. All this leads to a $7.50-8 per Mcf break even cost[1] but estimates are that gas prices will be around $3.75 for 2011.[2] Natural gas is a very volatile market with prices falling from $13 to $3 per Mcf in the space of a year from the summer of 2008.[3] Should global gas prices drop (for example because of a slow-down in the Chinese economy, an end to sanctions on Iran, or increased production in Iraq or off the Atlantic coasts of Brazil and Africa), then shale gas investments would quickly go bust.  And some analysts think that shale wells are likely to decline much more rapidly in productivity after an initial surge of gas than conventional wells are, meaning that rosy equations predicting long-term potential may be out by a factor of three.

[1] Dizard, John, ‘The true cost of shale gas production’, FT.com, 7 March 2010, http://www.ft.com/cms/s/0/e34f5282-2899-11df-a0b1-00144feabdc0.html#axzz...

[2] Buurma, Christine, ‘Raymond James Cuts 2011 Natural Gas Price Forecast to $3.75’, Bloomberg, 3 January 2011, http://www.bloomberg.com/news/2011-01-03/raymond-james-cuts-2011-natural...

[3] Krauss, Clifford, ‘Natural Gas Prices Plummet to a Seven-Year Low’, The New York Times, 20 August 2009, http://www.nytimes.com/2009/08/21/business/energy-environment/21gas.html

 

The world needs more energy.
Against
Point: 

Shale gas represents a very valuable new energy source for the USA and Europe and deserves government support. Demand is going to continue to increase the International Energy Agency forecasts that energy demand will grow by 36% between 2008 and 2035 with natural gas demand rising further, by 44%.[1] At a time when energy costs have been rising reaching near record prices even in the midst of low economic growth in the developed world,[2] and amid fears that the world is beginning to run out of oil and gas, fuel from shale gas can sustain our industrial society and hold down the cost of energy. Shale formations are also widespread and provide a domestic source of energy that will help America and Europe avoid over-reliance upon foreign fuel suppliers. Not only is this economically advantageous, but with most conventional oil and gas now supplied by potentially hostile or unstable states such as Iran, Iraq, Venezuela, Russia and Nigeria, it also strengthens our national security.

[1] International Energy Agency, ‘World Energy Outlook 2010 Factsheet’, iea.org, http://www.worldenergyoutlook.org/docs/weo2010/factsheets.pdf

[2] Espana, Zaida, ‘Oil seen over $100 in 2012 despite weak economy – Reuters poll’, Reuters, 28 September 2011, http://uk.reuters.com/article/2011/09/28/uk-energy-price-poll-idUKLNE78R...

 

Counterpoint: 

There is no great energy crisis that requires us to throw money at shale gas. U.S. and European oil supplies are now greatly diversified, with oil from Nigeria, Angola and Canada relieving the reliance America once had on the Middle East. The development of liquefied natural gas supply chains from a range of countries has had a similar impact in the gas market. The price of oil and gas is very variable, reflecting changing issues of supply and demand, so there is no reason to assume that current higher prices will last. It would be better for governments to focus on reducing energy consumption through greater efficiencies, and in diversifying away from fossil fuels as a category, by investment in renewables and nuclear power.

Shale Gas is good for the local economy
Against
Point: 

Shale gas should be welcomed as good for local economies. Many rural areas are extremely poor and suffering declining populations, with reduced employment opportunities as farming becomes increasingly mechanised and other industries move abroad. Through shale gas, landowners will gain lease payments from drilling companies, local workers will have new opportunities of well-paid employment, Marcellus the biggest shale field could have over 200,000 people working on it by 2020,[1] and businesses will benefit from this new influx of money. In addition, local governments will gain from increased tax revenues, for Marcellus these were $389 million in 2009,[2] allowing them to spend more on schools and services. And the prospect of cheap energy will encourage industry to relocate to these areas, while also holding down energy bills for local consumers.

[1] Considine, Timothy J., et al., ‘The Economic Impacts of the Pennsylvania Marcellus Shale Natural Gas Play: An Update’, Pennsylvania State University, 24 May 2010, p.iv, http://marcelluscoalition.org/wp-content/uploads/2010/05/PA-Marcellus-Up...

[2] Ibid, p.iv

 

Counterpoint: 

Shale gas contributes little to local economies and may actually damage them. Many of the economic benefits such as the use of hotel rooms are exaggerated due to studies not taking into account that they were being used before the gas industry arrived.[1] The few jobs it does create will go to skilled outsiders who will soon move on to the next project, so little of the money spent will reach local businesses and workers. Instead it may damage major local employers such as agriculture and tourism, as land is taken for drilling and its environmental impact deters visitors. House prices may be depressed by the proximity of heavy industry, construction traffic and the risks of pollution. And long-term clean-up costs may end up falling upon local taxpayers.

[1] NewScientist, ‘Economic benefits of shale-gas extraction unclear’, Issue 2819, 30 June 2011, http://www.newscientist.com/article/mg21128193.400-economic-benefits-of-shalegas-extraction-unclear.html

 

Fracking has been operating for years.
Against
Point: 

Hydraulic fracturing is not a new technique. It was pioneered as long ago as 1947[1] and had already been used a million times by 1988, so any problems or dangers have had decades to emerge. As they have not, we can be confident that shale gas production is safe. And the implication of banning these techniques, already used for 30% of all US oil and gas extraction, will not only wipe out the shale gas industry. As it is estimated 90% of current oil and gas wells in the USA will require fracking to maximise their potential,[2] a ban would have the effect of shutting down the whole American oil industry.

[1] Montgomery, Carl T., and Smith Michael B., ‘Hydraulic Fracturing History of an enduring technology’, JPT Online, Vol. 62, No. 12, December 2010, p.27  http://www.spe.org/jpt/print/archives/2010/12/10Hydraulic.pdf

[2] Batdorf, Tiffany, ‘National Registry Provides Public and Regulators Access to Information on Chemical Additives’, Interstate Oil & Gas Compact Commission, 11 April 2011http://www.iogcc.state.ok.us/national-registry-provides-public-and-regulators-access-to-information-on-chemical-additiv

 

Counterpoint: 

There is a difference between fracking which is occasionally used in standard drilling for oil and the much more regular use of fracking in order to extract shale gas. Most obviously it is more common. However there have also been significant changes in the technology. Fracking up to the end of the 1990’s used much less chemicals than current ‘slick water’ fracturing and fracturing is using about twice the amount of water as they were previously.[1] This should be enough change to mean there should be a halt to production until the science is certain. 

[1] Coal seam gas news, ‘Fracking, The DC Earthquake and Unclean Water’, 25th August 2011, http://coalseamgasnews.org/?p=1930
