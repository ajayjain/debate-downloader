This House believes that doctors should be allowed to prescribe contraception for under-age girls

Teenage pregnancy rates are on the rise, and contraceptives are often hailed as the underlying cause. However, few people agree on why contraceptives might be causing an increase in pregnancy rates. Some believe that a lack of contraceptives has led to more unprotected sex among young girls who choose to take that risk[1]; others claim that the availability of contraceptives encourages young girls to underestimate the emotional and physical impact of having sex at a young age[2]. It is very difficult to reach an absolute conclusion either way because of the difficulties of gathering data about whether underage girls have engaged in sexual activity. They may refuse to tell, or falsely say that they have been sexually active as a result of peer pressure to have sex[3].

            A high teenage pregnancy rate damages to the girls’ career or life prospects, and for society it often creates a financial and social burden to support the children and young mothers[4]. While doctors are bound by the principles of medical ethics to aid their patients as far as possible[5], providing contraceptives to under-age girls can be seen as undermining the purpose of the law against under-age intercourse because it provides a ‘safe’ way for them to break that law. It can also put doctors in an uncomfortable position if they feel that the girl will have intercourse without contraception if they refuse to prescribe it, at which point they may feel that they are failing to help the patient to avoid physical and mental health issues which could potentially result from unprotected sex.

            The mechanism for this debate is that doctors should be able to prescribe contraception to young girls, even if they have not yet reached the legal age of consent, as long as they satisfy certain conditions already laid out by medical authorities such as the British Medical Association. In this case, under the rules for informed consent, the patient must be ‘aware of’ and have ‘genuinely understood the hazards and benefits of various methods of contraception.’[6]  Doctors must also ‘make sure that patients are able to use the chosen method properly and can recognise and cope with side effects.’[7] This mechanism does not impose an absolute limit to contraceptive prescriptions based on age, but rather assesses each patient as an individual to ascertain whether that personally are ready for a sexual relationship. This upholds medical ethics in that the doctor’s concern should be ‘the welfare of the patient concerned’[8] – basing individual prescriptions on individual need – rather than imposing a uniform age restriction.

Clare Elliott
Refusing to prescribe contraceptives below a certain age ignores the principle of informed consent.
For
Point: 

Refusing to prescribe contraceptives for girls below the age of consent assumes that no under-age girls can make an informed decision to choose a sexual relationship. Medical advice and prescriptions should be given on an individual, rather than uniform, basis. As long as these girls meet the requirements for consent laid out by the relevant medical authority, there should be no reason for contraceptives should be denied to them. The decision of whether an individual is ready to engage in a sexual relationship is a purely personal one; refusing contraceptives places them at unnecessarily medical risk, both physically and mentally, and does not adhere to the duty of care by doctors for their patients[1].

[1] BBC. ‘Medical Ethics and Contraception’. Accessed from http://www.bbc.co.uk/ethics/contraception/ethics_contraception_1.shtml on 16/09/11.

 

Counterpoint: 

We have to place a limit somewhere. Though some children might be emotionally mature enough for sex at the age of 12 or 13, it is also destructive to imply that they all are. It can be very difficult to gauge whether a young girl ‘has genuinely understood’ the true consequences of her contraception, and giving routine prescriptions to under-age girls could give fuel to peer-pressure[1] as there is now no medical obstacle why the girl can refuse sex. ‘Informed consent’ is very difficult to gauge accurately, and the potential to misidentify a vulnerable girl who is being coerced as a girl who is fully informed could carry enormous consequences and fail to care for the best medical interests of the patient.

[1] Accessed from http://www.nhs.uk/Livewell/Sexandyoungpeople/Pages/Peerpressure.aspx on 16/09/11

 

Easily-available contraception does not increase teen sexual activity and can tackle teen pregnancy rates.
For
Point: 

Although it is almost impossible to absolutely prevent underage sex, as already shown by rising teen pregnancy rates in the US, the UK and across Europe[1][2], it is possible to encourage safe sex. Sex education programmes which teach abstinence rarely seem to deter sexual activity or prevent teen pregnancies and sexually transmitted infections[3]. However, studies researching the effect of providing contraceptives in schools have found that there was ‘no increase in sexual activity’[4]. If this principle was extended to include the many different types of contraception available by prescription, this could feasibly help to lower high teenage pregnancy rates; for example, the prescribed pill has a higher efficiency rate than condoms, which are often available in schools or clinics[5] but used inefficiently[6]. Devices like the IUD also remove any problems of girls forgetting to take their pills on time. [7] Allowing under-age girls to be able to talk to a doctor and receive the form of contraception which is best fitting for them[8] would actively tackle rising teen pregnancy rates.

Counterpoint: 

It is very difficult to gather accurate information over whether increased availability of contraception does or does not lead to increased sexual activity. However, the gesture of allowing underage girls to receive prescription from doctors is dangerous in principle. It represents the "systematic removal of every restraint that used to act as a disincentive to under-age sex"[1], and undermines the laws currently in place. Even if this does not have an immediate, visible, short-term effect on teen sexual activity, it may well lead to a disregard towards the need for emotional maturity in sex over the long-term.

Young girls should have the right to independent choice.
For
Point: 

The decision to have sex is an incredibly subjective one. Some girls might be ready at 13, others may not be ready until they are in their twenties or older. In fact, the age where girls enter puberty has become progressively younger; so, whereas 11 might be considered an average age to begin puberty, some girls can begin puberty as young as 7 years old[1]. However, in either case or early or of late development, the subjective decision of sexual maturity should not be permitted or outlawed by a system which takes no personal account of the individual; the only person who can judge if they are ready is the girl herself.

[1]           http://familydoctor.org/online/famdocen/home/children/parents/parents-teens/445.html

 

Counterpoint: 

Society frequently limits independent choice when there is a risk of harm to that individual or others. This is particularly true of young people because society recognises that these choices are not fully informed, reasoned, or rational at such a young age. This is also why you must reach a certain age to make decisions with substantial consequences such as getting married[1][2] or joining the army[3][4]. In cases such as these, where young girls’ bodies are still developing or they have just begun romantic relationships, they may not wholly understand their situation or the consequences and so cannot make a fully informed decision. This is even more evident if the girl would otherwise have unprotected sex rather than wait or use condoms, at physical and psychological risk to herself. It shows that they underestimate or misinterpret the long-term consequences of their actions.

Denying contraceptives negates a doctor’s ethical duty to help their patients.
For
Point: 

Disallowing doctors to prescribe contraceptives to young girls impedes their duties of medical ethics. The principle of beneficence instructs that doctors should do good for their patients[1]. If a young girl is in danger of an unplanned pregnancy and the potential medical complications which accompany it, a doctor should not deny her help if she asks for it and is medically suitable for the contraceptives. For example, she must be within the parameters for weight and blood pressure to take the combined pill, without undue likelihood of blood clots and other potential risks[2]. It is contradictory to give doctors the right to judge their patient’s best interests, but simultaneously prevent them from acting in their professional capacity.

[1]           http://www.stanford.edu/class/siw198q/websites/reprotech/New%20Ways%20of%20Making%20Babies/EthicVoc.htm, accessed 14/07/11

[2]           http://nhslocal.nhs.uk/my-health/conditions/c/combined-contraceptive-pill/special-considerations, accessed 14/07/11

 

Counterpoint: 

Medical ethics and the law clash here. Another important cornerstone of medical ethics is autonomy[1], which requires that the patient must be able to make a fully informed decision – something which might be compromised by her potential lack of maturity at a young age. It is particularly important that medical autonomy excludes ‘coercion or coaxing’[2] – this is placed in jeopardy from the risk of teenage peer pressure[3]. However, there is nothing to prevent a young girl from simply speaking to a doctor about her concerns, which would also fulfil the doctor’s ethical duty. Doctors must also operate within the boundaries of the law; if this motion was passed, the principles of law would be violated instead. This motion can only replace one negative effect – potential unprotected sex – with another, breaking the law. It is not a satisfactory solution.

[2]           http://www.stanford.edu/class/siw198q/websites/reprotech/New%20Ways%20of%20Making%20Babies/EthicVoc.htm, accessed 31/01/11

[3]           http://www.nhs.uk/Livewell/Sexandyoungpeople/Pages/Peerpressure.aspx, accessed 31/07/11

 

Easier access to prescribed contraceptives promotes information and education.
For
Point: 

By visiting the doctor, young girls have more access to information which they might not get from their school sex education. Given that, in the United States, there is ‘no federal mandate to teach sex education’ in public schools, and ‘less than half of all public schools . . . offer information on how to obtain birth control’[1], it is patently clear that the current educational system fails to address the needs of young adolescents in this respect. A doctor’s surgery or clinic is a more private environment for young girls to ask for clarification, without intimidation from male or female peers[2], which encourages education and makes sure that the girls are more fully informed before they have sex.

[1]           Naomi Starkman and Nicole Rajani. AIDS Patient Care and STDs. July 2002, 16(7): 313-318 at  http://www.liebertonline.com/doi/abs/10.1089/108729102320231144, accessed 30/07/11

[2]           http://www.nhs.uk/Livewell/Sexandyoungpeople/Pages/Peerpressure.aspx, accessed 31/07/11

 

Counterpoint: 

This is an argument to improve sex education, rather than to rely on doctors to provide outside information. If the school system of sex education is not working, then it should be improved, rather than diverting young girls elsewhere to try and find information. Furthermore, other methods of contraception are more easily available, such as condoms at drop-in clinics in the UK[1]. Speaking directly to a doctor, and requiring them to professionally sanction this underage sexual behaviour, is not the only way to obtain contraception.

[1]           http://www.nhs.uk/chq/Pages/839.aspx?CategoryID=54&SubCategoryID=128, accessed 31/07/11

 

Prescribing contraceptives for underage girls undermines the law and promotes illegal activity.
Against
Point: 

Prescribing contraceptives for underage sex shows an essential disregard for state law. By prescribing them, doctors essentially create a ‘safe’ way of breaking the law and justify the actions of these young girls. Even if it does not directly cause more young girls to become sexually active, it removes any deterrent which previously existed. This is dangerous as not only can it expose these girls to potential harm, but it gives a wider message that it is okay to ignore laws which are in place for peoples’ own protection. By allowing individuals to become an exception to the law, the wider system of state protection and social contract is at risk.

Counterpoint: 

These young people will likely ‘break’ the law anyway; however, providing them with contraception actually removes damage to the state by encouraging the safety of the young girls. It also helps to prevent unwanted teenage pregnancies which are a greater burden to the state, both financially and socially. In the UK, the cost of teen pregnancies is ‘estimated to be £63m a year’[1], while in the US taxpayer funds paid out ‘at least $10.9 billion in 2008’[2]. There are also instances where the state recognises that breaking the law is necessary for a greater good, for example self-defence is legal in the UK and US[3] when the victim is physically threatened and believes that they will suffer grievous harm if they do not act. While the circumstances are different, the principle is the same; laws are not absolute in cases where obeying the law would obviously cause a much greater harm than disobeying it.

Underage girls are particularly vulnerable and should be protected, not encouraged.
Against
Point: 

These girls are more likely to be both emotionally and physically vulnerable. Young girls’ relationships are less likely to be mature and stable than those over the age of consent; they are more at risk of coercion or pressure from their partners. For example, a 2009 report showed that 45% of teen girls knew a friend who had been ‘pressured into having either intercourse of oral sex’[1]. Indeed, this particular group of underage girls are not legally able to consent at all. Therefore, they form a particularly vulnerable group, and society has a duty of care towards them to prevent exploitation and harm. This is reflected by the fact that states grant minors predominantly protective rather than active rights, such as the right ‘survival’ without damage from others, and ‘to protection from harmful influences, abuse and exploitation’[2]. This continues until they have reached the age of majority or age of license where they can make mature decisions[3][4].

Contraceptives such as the pill or IUD also do not necessarily prevent other problems related to sex, such as STDs. Relaxing the law on this issue ultimately weakens the protection which we can give to these young girls; therefore it is damaging.

[2]           http://www.unicef.org/crc/, accessed 31/07/11

Counterpoint: 

The state has no right to objectively judge a subjective, personal relationship between two people. Many accepted adult relationships would not be identified as perfect, so it is unfair to impose this obligation on young people. 

A lack of contraception is not the primary reason for unprotected sex.
Against
Point: 

There are many possible reasons for a high rate of teenage pregnancies. Studies show that girls with ‘low educational expectations’ are ‘more likely to become sexually involved at an early age, to use contraception less regularly and effectively and to carry an unintended pregnancy to term’[1]. Many other socioeconomic factors can contribute to teen pregnancy. Furthermore, given that there is easy and free access to condoms from walk-in clinics[2] or even by buying them from shops, there is little indication that contraception is a key problem which contributes to rising teen pregnancy rates.

[1]           http://eurpub.oxfordjournals.org/content/7/2/144.full.pdf, accessed 14/07/11

[2]           http://www.nhs.uk/chq/Pages/839.aspx?CategoryID=54&SubCategoryID=128, accessed 31/07/11

 

Counterpoint: 

While there may be other factors which contribute, this doesn’t mean we shouldn’t try to solve this one. Providing more information and making contraceptives more accessible means that young girls have a better option to use them, even if social or economic factors make it more likely that they will be sexually active at a younger age. It is widely accepted that choosing which type of contraceptive to use is a very important and personal decision[1] depending on the individual’s health and circumstances. Some girls may feel more comfortable with the pill or an implant, which are not readily-available without a doctor’s appointment, and which are more effective than condoms alone[2]. So, while condoms might already be available, they may not always be right or appropriate for every girl.
