This House would allow capital punishment in international criminal law

The International Criminal Court does not use capital punishment, like its predecessors the International Criminal Tribunal for the Former Yugoslavia and the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda. The maximum sentence that can be imposed by the ICC is that of 30 years imprisonment, or, in some cases, life imprisonment. During the negotiations for the Rome Statute, a few nations, including Trinidad and Tobago and Egypt supported the death penalty under the Rome Statute1,2, however this was not accepted. Therefore, the most recent executions for international crimes were the executions in the post-WW2 trials at Nuremberg and Tokyo.

States that maintain capital punishment that are members of the International Criminal Court include Afghanistan, Japan, South Korea, Guyana, Trinidad and Tobago, Belize, Jordan and Botswana. ICC states parties do not practice the death penalty include South Africa, all member states in Europe and most in South America (two states are listed as abolitionist in practice by Amnesty International). However the fact that most ICC members do no support the death penalty domestically does not mean they should not in an international context, crimes being tried under international criminal law are often on a much greater scale than domestic offences. Capital punishment at the ICC could either be used as a regular occurrence, or be reserved for exceptional cases, as life imprisonment is – no person has been sentenced to life imprisonment by the ICC, five were by the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia and 21 by the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda.

Ultimate crimes, ultimate sanction.
For
Point: 

Genocide, crimes against humanity and war crimes are the most serious crimes in both international criminal law, and the criminal law of those states that have imported those in to their legal systems. For example in the US, the genocide and war crimes can be punishable by death1. As these crimes are on a larger scale than most domestic crimes often involving hundreds, thousands or potentially even millions of victims there should be similarly greater punishments as an option.

For this reason, the ultimate punishment, that of death, is appropriate and should be an option.

1 18 USC § 1091 http://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/1091 and 18 USC § 2441 http://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/2441

 

Counterpoint: 

In many legal systems, life imprisonment is considered the ultimate sanction, not death – as horrible as death is, it is not within the boundaries of an appropriate criminal sanction. Even if it were, a swift death is perhaps less of a punishment than languishing in a cell in a foreign land to consider, regret, and hopefully repent their actions. The existing direction of travel is one against the death penalty, so the argument that only death is a fitting sanction is out of step with the thinking of much of the world.

Respect for legal systems where it is used
For
Point: 

Domestic punishment of international crimes is a key principle of the Rome Statute. This is reflected by special concepts such as the use of universal jurisdiction (that a crime can be tried outside of the country in which it was committed), only for these crimes. If some international crimes are tried domestically and some internationally it makes sense for there to be all options for punishment available so that the penalties are the same. At the very least there should be the option for the death penalty for cases where the crime took place in, or where the victims were from, a country that has the death penalty.

Counterpoint: 

In reality, the death penalty is not used much, in many states, and it is particularly unpopular in states parties to the Rome Statute. Of the 122 state parties, only Afghanistan, Gambia, Japan, Bangladesh and Botswana, executed anyone in 20121 - there is no need to add the death penalty to match up with domestic sentences as ICC states parties in general do not use it.

1 Amnesty International, “Death Sentences and Executions 2012”, 2013, http://www.amnesty.org/en/library/asset/ACT50/001/2013/en/bbfea0d6-39b2-4e5f-a1ad-885a8eb5c607/act500012013en.pdf p48

 

Capital punishment is a deterrent
For
Point: 

While it is hard to carry out research on international criminal law, there is evidence that capital punishment can be a deterrent to serious crimes such as murder. A 1985 study by Stephen K. Layson of University of North Carolina, and a more recent one from Emory University have claimed that eighteen murders can be deterred by a single execution.1 It is clear that the bigger the crimes the more interest there should be in creating a deterrent effect as more lives will be saved. Having an international criminal court alone is not enough to create a deterrent, those who commit international crimes also need to know that they are going to face a tough penalty when tried.

1 Muhlhausen, David B., 'The Death Penalty Deters Crime and Saves Lives', The Heritage Foundation, 28 August 2007, http://www.heritage.org/research/testimony/the-death-penalty-deters-crime-and-saves-lives

 

Counterpoint: 

In addition to the disputability of assertions that capital punishment is a deterrent for ordinary crimes, it is unlikely that a significant increase in sentencing powers would actually be able to deter. An individual who commits such a serious crime would either not be concerned about sentencing as they think they would not be brought to trial, or would have no regard for what the consequences of their actions are.

The sentencing for international crimes is soft
For
Point: 

The lack of use of capital punishment for a crime resulting in death in a state which uses capital punishment effectively gives those convicted of a more serious crime a less serious sentence. International criminal courts (the ICC, ICTY and ICTR) have been accused of soft sentencing1. This is particularly stark when the ICC will only impose a sentence of over 30 years imprisonment in exceptional circumstance2 – even many countries like the UK, which abolished the death penalty in 1969 following a moratorium in 1965, utilizes a mandatory life sentence for murder3.

1 Jelinić, Berislav, 'Kevin Parker – The judge who freed the villains of Vukovar', Nacional, no.620, 10 February 2007, http://www.nacional.hr/en/clanak/38490/kevin-parker-the-judge-who-freed-the-villains-of-vukovar

2 Rome Statute, Article 77 http://legal.un.org/icc/statute/romefra.htm

3 Murder (Abolition of the Death Penalty) Act 1965 [United Kingdom], s1(1). http://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1965/71

 

Counterpoint: 

Not having the death penalty does not make sentencing under international criminal law soft. It is wrong to consider the death penalty to be a harder punishment than a a long prison sentence. While some crimes may demand a true life sentence we should remember that international criminal law usually is not trying young people as such people have not had the chance to attain the kind or power that enables such crimes. Thus thirty years will amount to a life sentence for many and will carry the rest into old age.  

ICC use of capital punishment would alienate many European and South American states
Against
Point: 

The European Union has provided the political impetus for the International Criminal Court, and its member states are some of the largest contributors to the court’s budget.

There is a clear European consensus against capital punishment. All EU member states, and all of the other members of the Council of Europe, have abolished the death penalty, or established a moratorium (Belarus is neither a member of the European Union or the Council of Europe). All Council of Europe members apart from Russia have ratified Protocol 6 of the European Convention on Human Rights which prohibits the death penalty in peacetime, and all bar three have signed and ratified (including all European Union members) Protocol 13 of the Convention, which prohibits it in all circumstances. Many European states, such as the United Kingdom have strategies for the worldwide abolition of capital punishment.1

Similarly, there is a near consensus against the death penalty in South America. All South American nations are members of the International Criminal Court. And, with the exception of Guyana, who is listed as maintaining the death penalty by Amnesty International2, have abolished the death penalty.

Introduction of capital punishment by the ICC may well cause a large number of member states to withdraw from the ICC as it would embody the antithesis of their worldwide human rights policies.

1 Foreign & Commonwealth Office, 'HMG Strategy for Abolition of the Death Penalty 2010-2015', gov.uk, October 2011, https://www.gov.uk/government/uploads/system/uploads/attachment_data/file/35448/death-penalty-strategy-oct-11-15.pdf

2 Amnesty International, “Death Sentences and Executions 2012”, 2013, http://www.amnesty.org/en/library/asset/ACT50/001/2013/en/bbfea0d6-39b2-4e5f-a1ad-885a8eb5c607/act500012013en.pdf

 

Counterpoint: 

In many cases, states that do not use capital punishment will co-operate with those that do. For example, individuals can be extradited from the United Kingdom to the USA, as long as capital punishment is ruled out in those individual cases. Such arrangements could be used in the International Criminal Court; they show that these states are not opposed to capital punishment to an extent where they will completely refuse to assist a system of justice. Allowing the death penalty in some cases would be an option without causing a mass withdrawal.

Capital punishment is too expensive
Against
Point: 

International criminal justice is already expensive as it is. However, capital punishment makes criminal justice systems even more costly. North Carolina’s system costs over ten million US dollars per year more than life imprisonment1, a cost roughly at two million dollars per execution. This is because of the greater necessity for an extensive appeals system to check and double check against the possibility of a miscarriage of justice. In international justice it would be even more important to prevent miscarriages of justice. At the same time an increase the cost of the system is not desirable at a time of worldwide financial constraint. This would be likely to lead to even more under-resourcing of the prosecutor, rendering it even harder to for the ICC to actually do its job.

1 Cook, Philip, “Potential Savings from Abolition of the Death Penalty in North Carolina”, American Law and Economics Review, 2009, http://m.deathpenaltyinfo.org/documents/CookCostRpt.pdf

 

Counterpoint: 

Justice is not something that can have a price applied to it. If the appropriate punishment is expensive, so be it. If the elevated cost is due to the appeals process, which is already lengthy under the Rome Statute, that can be dealt with by reducing the prospects for appeals. This will only apply to a very small number of cases, as the ICC investigates and convicts a small number of people, and would not execute all of them so the total cost is not going to be high.

International law should not sanction human rights violations
Against
Point: 

Capital punishment is a violation of the right to life, as enshrined by the Universal Declaration of Human Rights1. This is explicitly accepted in a variety of international treaties including the European Convention on Human Rights protocols 6 and 13, the Charter of Fundamental rights of the European Union, the Second Optional Protocol to the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights.

This is reflected by the UN General Assembly voting for a worldwide moratorium on the death penalty three times in 2007, 2008 and 2010.

1 Amnesty International, “Death Penalty“, no date, http://www.amnesty.org/en/death-penalty

 

Counterpoint: 

There is no international consensus on this issue. Many advanced democracies, such as South Korea, Japan and the USA, use capital punishment. It is up to each individual state to determine its interpretations of the exact contours of human rights.

At any rate, those who commit such grave and inhuman acts, recognised as unacceptable worldwide, can be argued to have forfeited such human rights – their actions are against the world as a whole rather than mere individuals. 

Would accentuate victors justice arguments
Against
Point: 

The international criminal justice system has already been criticized by some for being an exercise in victor’s justice1. This could be an even more trenchant criticism if capital punishment were to be instituted. Having the threat of the death penalty would help ensure that the accused will not be willing to hand themselves over or take part in trials as Kenyatta and Ruto of Kenya have. With those who are executed it would add the effect of turning these international criminals into martyrs for their particular causes, potentially harming prospects for long term peace. While they are alive there is always the possibility that they may help atone for their crimes by encouraging their forces and allies to disarm and take part in encouraging peace and stability.

1 See Girard, Raphael, 'The Perception of “Victor's Justice” in the International Criminal Tribunals for the Former Yugoslavia and Rwanda', Legal Frontiers, 14 March 2012, http://www.legalfrontiers.ca/2012/03/the-perception-of-%E2%80%9Cvictor%E2%80%99s-justice%E2%80%9D-in-the-international-criminal-tribunals-for-the-former-yugoslavia-and-rwanda/

 

Counterpoint: 

Many of the post Second World War trial convicts were executed. There has not been much particular focus on those executed in Nuremberg as martyrs; that has focused on the actual leadership. If anything, the executions helped bring finality to the situation rather than inflame matters – a dead person cannot be subsequently released and return as leader to a cause.
