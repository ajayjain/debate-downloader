This house would subsidise the arts

Despite the fact that the particular circumstances of government funding for the arts will vary from country to country and should be researched carefully, the principles for this debate remain universal. In an age of global economic difficulty the fundamental question is whether or not public money in any form should subsidise arts projects which are not self-sufficient. In looking at this question, 'the arts' as a term should include museums and galleries as well as theatre, literature, music and film - all of these things are currently supported by government funding in the UK.1 As well as looking at whether or not these projects should receive any money at all, there is also the question of what form of subsidy these projects should take - for example, is funding via a state lottery more acceptable than direct grants from the national treasury? Ultimately any decision will be based in no small part on how far each individual values the concept of cultural output over more pragmatically valuable areas - whether or not people feel that in the modern world it is justifiable to use the limited resources available to not only foster new artistic projects, but also to protect the cultural heritage of the past. There is a natural comparison within this question between the European and American systems. In Europe the majority of funding comes from government grants, where as in America there is a more competitive atmosphere in which projects must win favour with wealthy citizens and businesses who donate private funds. Even in the United States some money is given by the federal government through the National Endowment for the Arts which gave $139million in 2010.2 The director of the Tate Modern, Sir Nicholas Serota, argues that "the key is the American culture of giving. People who have made money there are expected to give some of it back to society"3 It has however been suggested that it would be impossible to implement such a system in Europe,4 and if that is indeed the case, the emphasis must shift to how far that system could reinforce the established governmental policies in Europe.

1 Arts Council England, accessed 7/7/11 [1]
2 NEA At A Glance, National Endowment for the Arts, accessed 21/7/11 [2]
3 Andrew Clark, 2007, accessed 7/7/11 [3]
4 Will Gompertz, 2010, accessed 31/5/11 [4]

Money spent on arts projects does not always return profit or positive press.
Against
Point: 

There are many instances of government money being wasted on art that is unpopular, such as the purchase of the widely criticised statue 'Santa Claus' (a work by the American artist Paul McCarthy) by Rotterdam's local government in 2003.1 Indeed, the amount of what is seen by most to be 'bad public art' in Rotterdam prompted the organisation of a one-off tour by the Centre for Visual Arts (CBK) in May of 2009 which showed the least successful arts projects in the city. Although there are some positive effects of the increased publicity created by bad public art, this is outweighed by the fact that money spent on these projects could be spent on things like schools, pensions and healthcare, all of which are also affected by the current economic problems and a lack of funding.

1 Public Art Network, 2009, accessed 31/5/11 [5]

Counterpoint: 

There are clear economic benefits to the subsidy of the arts. Looking beyond the basic profit/loss system of valuation, it is impossible to quantify the positive effects of government-backed companies such as the National Theatre or Royal Shakespeare Company which in Britain act as centres of excellence, training and nurturing young talent and thus helping to support a thriving commercial artistic sector as well. They create talent which operates in the commercial market, making money from the art they produce as well as attracting tourist to parts of the country that would not otherwise receive it. An example of this economic benefit can be seen in Newcastle and Gateshead in the north of England, where according to the Treasury's own calculations every £1 of public investment in the 10 main cultural buildings results in £5 generated by the regional economy1.

1BBC News, 2011(b), accessed 31/5/11.

The cutting of arts subsidies results in a reduction in artistic output of all kinds.
For
Point: 

If we accept the term 'arts' to cover museums, galleries, theatres and musical and film projects, then it is clear that without public funding in many countries a large proportion of arts organisations would be lost. Taking the UK as a prime example of what happens when funding for the arts is cut, we can see that of the 1333 companies which applied for funding from Arts Council England in 2010 only 695 were successful. A cut of £100M has meant this number has dropped from the 849 organisations that were granted funding in the previous application process1. The Arts Council is responsible for the funding of cultural venues, theatres, galleries and performing arts groups, and this cut to its budget demonstrates the depth of repercussions that occur when the arts cease to be subsidised. There is also evidence to suggest that the American system of philanthropic donations simply doesn’t work in the UK, as investment in the arts by the business sector was shown in figures released in January 2011 to have fallen by 11%2.

1Alan Davey, 2011, accessed 31/5/11.

2BBC News, 2011, accessed 31/5/11.

Counterpoint: 

There are alternatives to public funding - the arts have relied on patronage and donations for a very long time, and there are examples of a positive relationship between the arts and business enterprises. There could be better utilisation of this relationship - America is a prime example of this. Although there are organisations set up in America to provide money for the arts such as The National Endowments for Arts set up in 1965,1 they rely far more on donations from businesses and wealthy individuals than companies do in the UK and Europe. Given the cuts to public spending especially in the UK, an introduction of American philanthropic ideas is a viable means to bridge the gap in funding caused by the economic situation. As well as this, it is important to note that of the 695 organisations given grants, 110 of them were new projects. This shows that even in a time of economic strife, the limited resources available can be deployed in such a way that art and culture remains up-to-date and that new art can still be produced.

1 National Endowment for the Arts, accessed 31/5/11 [6]

There are organisations and projects already running that provide a means for artists and potential buyers to produce, sell and buy art at a much lower price than the established convention.
Against
Point: 

The Affordable Art Fair, which during the recession has seen sales drop by just 5% compared to 10-15% at the more expensive end of the spectrum, gives artists a chance to showcase their work in what are hard times for painters, sculptors and photographers.1 Dependence on government funding can result in distortion of artistic output to fulfil the requirements set out by the governing body. A desire to maintain that funding can, and often has in the past resulted in the twisting of art to become little more than propaganda, or at least less than what it could have potentially been had a political agenda not been an influence. The cutting of organisations that previously received money does not negate any positive work they achieved in the past - not ever project can last forever, and when they come to an end for whatever reason the focus should be on their achievements not their loss.

1 Burgess, 2009, accessed 31/5/11 [7]

Counterpoint: 

Arts subsidies allow companies to take risks. Rather than being forced into producing what has proven to be popular in the past as commercial companies have to do to ensure their success, the subsidies provided by the government enable new styles and forms of art to emerge and to become popular in their own right. By taking away that funding, not only will it reduce the overall number of artistic ventures and organisations, it will also reduce the amount of new art being created. The current cuts to the English Arts Council have resulted in money that has previously been spent on projects like the Asian Music Circuit being wasted as they are shut down. The Independent reported from chief executive Viram Jasani that "We were actually set up at the Arts Council's behest in the 1980s, so this decision doesn't make any sense at all."1

1 Rob Sharp, 2011, accessed 31/5/11 [8]

Money from the National Lottery is essential to arts programmes, just as it is to many other organisations in the UK.
For
Point: 

Since the lottery began in Britain in1994 over £25 billion has been given to ‘good causes’. Last year (2010) 16.67% of the donations made were to arts organisations, the same amount that was given to sport and heritage projects1. This money makes up an significant part of many organisation’s budgets, and stopping this funding whilst still giving to sports and heritage foundations would be discriminatory and unfair. Equal distribution of this public lottery is the only way to ensure that people are being fairly treated when they buy their lottery ticket, even though the ‘good causes’ their money goes to may not be their primary concern.

1The National Lottery, accessed 31/5/11.

Counterpoint: 

In the same year (2010), 50% of the donations made by the National Lottery in the UK were to Health, Education, Environment, and charitable causes. This in itself demonstrates an awareness of the need to prioritise public spending into more socially and economically important areas. On the subject of fairness, it would be difficult to argue that the same number of people benefit from donations to the arts as they do from donations to health, education and charitable organisations. Even if buyers of lottery tickets do not think about where their money goes, if they were to do so they would be happier to see it go to these areas rather than to arts projects that are beneficial only to a select group.

There would be a social, as well as an economic impact were public spending to the arts to be cut.
For
Point: 

The reduction in available services would make arts less accessible to all, and although the demand would still be there, forcing companies to ensure they make a profit will drive ticket prices up and prevent those from poorer backgrounds from being able to enjoy the same cultural experiences as those who are wealthy. Subsidy can ensure that cheaper tickets are available and that the arts remain democratically open to all, regardless of income or social class. It also allows other public institutions which have similar economic problems such as schools and colleges to provide better services for their students, giving them access to better teaching resources and therefore a better quality of education.1 It would be unfair and inaccurate to assume that the benefits of arts subsidy only go to the wealthy elite, as the focus is on providing arts projects for all members of society. Free and cheap access to the arts is crucial for education. Without subsidy schools and young people would not be able to take affordable music lessons, visit museums or galleries, or to attend plays or concerts, and would thereby be prevented from understanding and enjoying their culture fully. As well as being important for personal enrichment, access to the arts also makes the young aware of their cultural heritage both nationally and in world terms, increasing the likelihood of continued interest in the arts and its creation in the future.

1 Murray, 2010, accessed 31/5/11 [9]

Counterpoint: 

Other, equally deserving public leisure activities either do not need to, or simply do not receive any such funding - why is is that the arts are deemed to have greater importance, especially given that they are proven to not be self-sufficient. Certain art forms, such as live music events and special exhibitions charge entrance fees and people are willing to pay them. Looking at it from the perspective of what art actually means, if people are not willing to pay to support it, then it has surely failed in its purpose to provide something culturally relevant. Brian Micklethwait of the Libertarian Alliance argues that good art can survive economic difficulties,1 and that if the arts really are as important in society as some people say, then the public would be willing to spend money to view it, just as they do for a concert or a football match.

1 Micklethwait, 1983, accessed 31/5/11 [10]
